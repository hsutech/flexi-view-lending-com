Private Lending for Investors: What to Prepare

Start with the investment thesis

Private lending is easier to review when the borrower can explain the deal in plain language. What is being purchased, why does the price make sense, what work is needed, who will complete it and how will the loan be repaid? A clear thesis does not need hype. It needs numbers, dates and an honest description of the property.

A practical borrower can use this section as a checklist. Write down the answer in plain language, attach the document that proves it when possible and identify anything that is still uncertain. That habit does not make a difficult deal easy, but it prevents the review from drifting into guesswork.

The same discipline also protects the borrower. A loan that looks fast can become expensive if the exit is vague, the budget is thin or the repayment event depends on an assumption nobody has tested. Before choosing a loan type, compare the request with the related service pages and decide whether the product name matches the real timing problem.

Documents that reduce friction

Borrowers should gather the purchase contract, settlement statement if available, entity documents, operating agreement, borrower identification, bank statements, insurance contact, property photos, rent roll, leases and repair budget. For a refinance, current loan statements and payoff letters matter. For a rehab or construction request, contractor estimates and a schedule of values matter.

Experience and execution

Lenders do not only review the asset. They review the borrower's ability to execute. Experience can be shown through completed projects, current portfolio summaries, contractor relationships, management systems and a realistic budget. Newer investors can still present a strong file, but they should be especially clear about who is helping them and how decisions will be made.

Exit strategy

The exit strategy is the backbone of a private lending request. If the plan is resale, comparable sales and selling costs should be realistic. If the plan is refinance, rent, income and stabilization details should support the expected permanent loan. If the plan is payoff from another transaction, that transaction needs evidence.
